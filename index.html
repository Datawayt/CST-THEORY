Critical Symmetry Theory

A Rigorous Mathematical Framework for the Riemann Hypothesis

1. Fundamental Premise

Critical Symmetry Theory (CST) proposes a geometric and dynamical framework to solve the Riemann Hypothesis by focusing on three primary mechanisms that govern the distribution of the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function.

The Riemann Hypothesis suggests that all non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function lie on the line Re(s) = 1/2. CST connects this hypothesis to an advanced geometric structure and dynamical processes that dictate the behaviour of these zeros.

Geometric Constraint

The Riemann zeta function ζ(s) is embedded within a curved Kähler manifold, where the critical line (Re(s) = 1/2) is represented as the only stable geodesic. This introduces a deep interaction between the geometry of the manifold and the analytic properties of ζ(s), constraining the zeros to the critical line.

Dynamic Enforcement

Through a process known as Zeta Flow, the function ζ(s) is subject to a dynamical system that minimises |ζ(s)|². This dynamic approach strengthens the notion that the non-trivial zeros can be driven towards the critical line, reinforcing the idea that they lie on Re(s) = 1/2.

Symmetry Principle

In CST, a Zeta-Mirror duality exists, a symmetry that acts to annihilate non-critical zeros of ζ(s), ensuring that only those that lie on the critical line remain. This duality mirrors the structure of the complex plane in a way that eliminates extraneous solutions.

2. Geometric Formulation

The geometric formulation of CST requires constructing a Kähler manifold M that encodes the properties of the Riemann zeta function ζ(s) within its curvature.

2.1 Kähler Manifold Construction

The central object in CST is a 2D complex manifold M, which is endowed with a Kähler metric. This metric is defined in such a way that the critical line of ζ(s) (Re(s) = 1/2) becomes a minimal geodesic. The construction is crucial as it geometrically constrains the non-trivial zeros of ζ(s) to this line.

The metric g_{iȷ̄} = ∂_i∂_ȷ̄ log|ζ(s)|² describes the curvature of the manifold, and its determinant behaves in a special way, blowing up at the zeros of ζ(s), ensuring that these points are geometrically “attracted” to the critical line.

2.2 Curvature-Driven Attraction

The scalar curvature R of the manifold creates potential wells, leading to the attraction of the non-trivial zeros of ζ(s) towards the critical line. The potential for this attraction is given by the expression:

V(s) = ¼R - ½Δ_g log|ζ(s)|

This is essential for understanding the behaviour of the zeros under the influence of the manifold's curvature. The resulting flow can be described by the following equation:

∇_g V · t > 0 ∀ t ∦ critical line

3. Zeta Flow Dynamics

The dynamics of CST involve a gradient descent process that minimizes the squared modulus of ζ(s). This process is represented by the Zeta Flow equation, which describes the evolution of ζ(s) over time.

3.1 Flow Equation Derivation

The flow is driven by a gradient descent algorithm that minimises |ζ(s)|², which is the magnitude of the Riemann zeta function. This gives rise to the following equation for the flow of s:

ds/dτ = -g^{iȷ̄} (∂_{ȷ̄}|ζ(s)|²) ∂_i

This equation represents the time evolution of the complex variable s under the flow, where τ is the time parameter. It expresses how the points on the complex plane move under the influence of the Zeta Flow.

3.2 Stability Analysis

Linearising the system around the critical line Re(s) = 1/2, we obtain the Jacobian matrix of the flow:

J = -2|ζ(s)|² I + O(σ - ½)

All eigenvalues of this matrix are negative definite for σ ≠ ½, ensuring that the flow drives the system towards stability along the critical line.

4. Verification Protocol

To verify the correctness of CST, we require both analytic and computational methods. The following sections outline the protocol for validating the theory.

4.1 Analytic Proofs Required

  • Global existence of Zeta Flow solutions, ensuring that the flow always converges and does not encounter singularities.
  • Completeness of the Kähler metric, ensuring that the manifold is smooth and well-behaved across the entire complex plane.
  • Spectral gap analysis for the Hamiltonian operator Ĥ that governs the dynamics of ζ(s), ensuring that the flow leads to a stable configuration with no other competing critical lines.

4.2 Computational Verification
